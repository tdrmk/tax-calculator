# How Federal & California Taxes Are Calculated

For someone with W-2 income, stock gains, bond interest, and savings interest.

---

## Step 1 — Classify Your Income Into Two Buckets

Not all income is taxed the same way. You need to split everything into two buckets:

**Bucket A — Ordinary Income** (taxed at regular rates):
- Your **W-2 wages/salary**
- **Short-term capital gains** (stocks held ≤ 1 year)
- **Ordinary (non-qualified) dividends**
- **Bond interest** (coupon payments)
- **Savings account / money market interest**

**Bucket B — Preferential Income** (gets special lower federal rates):
- **Long-term capital gains** (stocks held > 1 year)
- **Qualified dividends** (dividends that meet the holding period requirement)

**Total income** = everything from both buckets combined.

---

## Step 2 — Subtract Pre-Tax Deductions to Get AGI

Pre-tax deductions (401k, HSA, health insurance premiums, etc.) reduce your income before taxes are calculated:

> **AGI (Adjusted Gross Income)** = Total Income − Pre-Tax Deductions

For example, if you make $200,000 total and contribute $23,500 to a 401(k) and $4,300 to an HSA, your AGI = $200,000 − $23,500 − $4,300 = **$172,200**.

---

## Step 3 — Federal Tax on Ordinary Income

1. **Start with AGI**
2. **Subtract the federal standard deduction** ($15,750 for Single in 2025, $31,500 for MFJ)
3. **Subtract your preferential income** (long-term capital gains + qualified dividends)

> **Federal Ordinary Taxable Income** = AGI − Standard Deduction − LTCG − Qualified Dividends

4. **Apply the progressive brackets** to this amount. The key concept: each bracket only applies to the dollars *within that range*, not your entire income.

For a Single filer in 2025:
- First $11,925 → taxed at **10%**
- Next chunk ($11,926 – $48,475) → taxed at **12%**
- Next chunk ($48,476 – $103,350) → taxed at **22%**
- Next chunk ($103,351 – $197,300) → taxed at **24%**
- …and so on up to **37%**

So if your ordinary taxable income is $80,000, you don't pay 22% on all of it — you pay 10% on the first slice, 12% on the next, and 22% only on the portion above $48,475.

---

## Step 4 — Federal Tax on Preferential Income (LTCG & Qualified Dividends)

Long-term capital gains and qualified dividends get their own, lower tax rates: **0%, 15%, or 20%**.

The trick is **stacking**: imagine your ordinary taxable income fills up the brackets first, and then your preferential income sits on top. The rate for the preferential income depends on where it "lands" in the overall income stack.

**Example (Single):**
- Your ordinary taxable income is $40,000 (fills up the stack to $40,000)
- You have $20,000 in LTCG
- The 0% LTCG bracket goes up to $48,350 for Single
- So the first $8,350 of your LTCG ($40,000 → $48,350) is taxed at **0%**
- The remaining $11,650 ($48,351 → $60,000) is taxed at **15%**

---

## Step 5 — Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)

This is an additional **3.8%** surtax on investment income if you're a higher earner. It applies when your AGI exceeds **$200,000** (Single) or **$250,000** (MFJ).

The tax base is the **lesser of**:
- Your total net investment income (all capital gains + dividends + interest), OR
- The amount your AGI exceeds the threshold

**Example:** AGI is $230,000 (Single) with $50,000 of investment income.
- Excess over threshold = $230,000 − $200,000 = $30,000
- Investment income = $50,000
- NIIT base = lesser of $30,000 and $50,000 = **$30,000**
- NIIT = $30,000 × 3.8% = **$1,140**

---

## Step 6 — Total Federal Income Tax

> **Total Federal Tax** = Ordinary income tax + LTCG/qualified dividend tax + NIIT (if applicable)

---

## Step 7 — California State Tax

California is simpler in one important way: **it taxes ALL income as ordinary income**. There is no preferential rate for long-term capital gains or qualified dividends. Stocks you held for 10 years? Same rate as your salary.

1. **Start with AGI** (same AGI as federal)
2. **Subtract the CA standard deduction** ($5,706 for Single, $11,412 for MFJ) — much smaller than federal
3. **Apply CA progressive brackets** to the entire taxable amount:
   - 1%, 2%, 4%, 6%, 8%, 9.3%, 10.3%, 11.3%, 12.3%
   - Plus an extra **1% Mental Health Services Tax** on income over $1,000,000 (making the top rate **13.3%**)

Same marginal/progressive concept as federal — each bracket only applies to the dollars within that range.

---

## Step 8 — Payroll Taxes (FICA) — Wages Only

These apply **only to W-2 wages**, not investment income. The wage base for FICA is your gross wages minus health insurance premiums and certain other pre-tax deductions (but NOT minus retirement contributions like 401k — those are still subject to FICA).

- **Social Security:** 6.2% on wages up to **$176,100** per person
- **Medicare:** 1.45% on **all** wages
- **Additional Medicare:** 0.9% on wages exceeding **$200,000** (Single) / **$250,000** (MFJ)

---

## Step 9 — CA SDI (State Disability Insurance) — Wages Only

- **1.2%** on all wages (same wage base as FICA — no cap)
- Does not apply to investment income

---

## Putting It All Together

| Component | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| Federal ordinary tax | W-2 income + short-term gains + bond/savings interest + ordinary dividends |
| Federal LTCG tax | Long-term capital gains + qualified dividends (lower rates, stacked) |
| NIIT | 3.8% surtax on investment income if AGI exceeds threshold |
| California tax | ALL income taxed as ordinary (no preferential rates) |
| Social Security | 6.2% on wages only, capped at $176,100 |
| Medicare | 1.45% + 0.9% additional on wages only |
| CA SDI | 1.2% on wages only |

> **Total Tax** = Federal ordinary + Federal LTCG + NIIT + California + Social Security + Medicare + Additional Medicare + CA SDI

> **Effective Tax Rate** = Total Tax ÷ Total Income

> **Take-Home Pay** = Total Income − All Taxes − Pre-Tax Deductions − Post-Tax Contributions

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Ordinary income and preferential income are taxed differently at the federal level** — holding stocks for over a year gets you lower rates
2. **California doesn't care** — it taxes everything the same
3. **FICA/SDI only hit your wages**, not your investment income
4. **NIIT is the "gotcha"** for high-earning investors — it adds 3.8% on top of everything else
5. **Everything is progressive/marginal** — moving into a higher bracket only affects the dollars in that bracket, not all your income

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **AGI (Adjusted Gross Income)** | Your total income minus pre-tax deductions. The starting point for calculating both federal and state taxes. |
| **Standard Deduction** | A fixed dollar amount the IRS/state lets you subtract from AGI before applying tax brackets. You get this automatically without itemizing. |
| **Progressive / Marginal Brackets** | A system where income is taxed in slices — each slice (bracket) has its own rate. Only the dollars within a bracket are taxed at that bracket's rate. |
| **Marginal Tax Rate** | The rate applied to your last (highest) dollar of income. This is the bracket you're currently "in." |
| **Effective Tax Rate** | Your total tax divided by your total income — the blended average rate you actually pay. Always lower than your marginal rate. |
| **Ordinary Income** | Income taxed at regular federal rates (10%–37%): wages, short-term gains, bond interest, savings interest, non-qualified dividends. |
| **Preferential Income** | Income that qualifies for lower federal rates (0%/15%/20%): long-term capital gains and qualified dividends. |
| **Capital Gains** | Profit from selling an asset (e.g., stocks) for more than you paid. **Short-term** (held ≤ 1 year) = ordinary rates. **Long-term** (held > 1 year) = preferential rates. |
| **Qualified Dividends** | Dividends from stocks held long enough to meet IRS holding period requirements. Taxed at the lower LTCG rates instead of ordinary rates. |
| **Ordinary Dividends** | Dividends that don't meet the qualified holding period. Taxed as ordinary income. |
| **LTCG (Long-Term Capital Gains)** | Gains on assets held longer than one year. Taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% federally, depending on your total taxable income. |
| **Stacking** | The method for determining LTCG tax rates — ordinary income fills the brackets first, then preferential income "stacks" on top to find its applicable rate. |
| **NIIT (Net Investment Income Tax)** | A 3.8% federal surtax on investment income (gains, dividends, interest) for high earners whose AGI exceeds $200K (Single) or $250K (MFJ). |
| **FICA** | Federal Insurance Contributions Act — the combination of Social Security and Medicare taxes withheld from your paycheck. Only applies to wages, not investment income. |
| **Social Security Tax** | 6.2% tax on wages up to $176,100 per person (2025). Funds Social Security retirement benefits. |
| **Medicare Tax** | 1.45% tax on all wages, plus an additional 0.9% on wages above $200K (Single) / $250K (MFJ). Funds Medicare health coverage. |
| **FICA Wages** | The wage amount subject to Social Security and Medicare taxes: gross wages minus health insurance and certain other pre-tax deductions, but *not* minus retirement contributions. |
| **CA SDI (State Disability Insurance)** | California's 1.2% payroll tax on all wages (no cap). Funds short-term disability and paid family leave benefits. |
| **Pre-Tax Deductions** | Amounts subtracted from your paycheck before taxes are calculated — 401(k), HSA, health premiums, etc. Reduces your taxable income. |
| **W-2** | The tax form your employer sends you showing your wages and taxes withheld. Different boxes report different wage definitions (Box 1 = federal taxable, Box 3 = Social Security wages, etc.). |
| **Gross Pay (Gross Wages)** | Your total compensation from your employer before any deductions or taxes are taken out. This is your W-2 salary/wages only — it does **not** include investment income. |
| **Gross Income / Total Income** | Gross pay **plus** all investment income (capital gains, dividends, bond interest, savings interest). The "everything combined" number before any deductions. This is what feeds into the AGI calculation. The IRS uses "Total Income" on Form 1040. |
| **Filing Status** | Your tax category — Single, Married Filing Jointly (MFJ), Married Filing Separately (MFS), or Head of Household (HoH). Determines your bracket thresholds and standard deduction. |

### A Note on "Gross"

The word **"gross"** in tax terminology simply means **"before anything is subtracted."** The IRS uses "gross income" (or "total income") on Form 1040 to mean all income from all sources combined — wages, capital gains, dividends, interest, etc. — before any deductions. The flow is:

> **Gross Income** = everything you earned from all sources before deductions
> → minus pre-tax deductions
> = **Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)**
> → minus standard deduction
> = **Taxable Income**
